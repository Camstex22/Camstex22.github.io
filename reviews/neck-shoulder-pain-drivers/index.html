Why Neck and Shoulder Pain Hits Drivers Harder Than Anyone Else

Most occupational health research focuses on desk workers. But a 2023 systematic review published in the Journal of Occupational and Environmental Medicine found that professional drivers — including long-haul truckers, last-mile delivery drivers, and gig-economy couriers — experience musculoskeletal neck and shoulder disorders at rates up to 2.4 times higher than sedentary office workers. The reasons are specific, compound, and almost never addressed by generic pain relief products.

Consider what your neck and shoulder system endures across a single 10-hour haul or a full block of delivery shifts. You're performing between 800 and 1,200 head-rotation checks — mirrors, blind-spot checks, navigation glances — each one creating a brief eccentric load on your sternocleidomastoid and upper trapezius. Your shoulders grip the steering wheel with an average isometric force of 15–35 lbs across mountain roads, urban traffic, and dock manoeuvring. Whole-body vibration from road surface transmits directly through the seat, up the spine, and dissipates in the cervical vertebrae and shoulder joints. And because most long-haul drivers sleep in their cab on irregular schedules, their muscles rarely complete a full repair cycle before the next shift begins. This is a specific, cumulative injury pattern — not a simple stiff neck.

Delivery drivers in gig and last-mile networks face a different but overlapping set of stressors. Constant stop-start driving means repeated braking-force loads through the shoulders. Craning toward tablet navigation mounts — often positioned at awkward angles in vans and box trucks — forces the head into sustained forward flexion. Package lifting compounds rotator cuff wear. And the pressure of completing 150+ stops per block means drivers almost never get the recovery breaks that would let cervical tension release naturally. When you're looking for a solution, you need something that works within your life as it actually is: no gym, no physiotherapy appointments on the road, no consistent sleep schedule, and no time for lengthy recovery protocols during a busy week.
